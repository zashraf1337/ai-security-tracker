1. Executive summary

This report captures a comprehensive initial survey of startups at the intersection of AI and cybersecurity. It covers 83 active companies across 12 sub-categories, summarizes notable funding rounds and exits from 2024 through Q1 2026, distills recurring themes and contrarian approaches, and proposes fifteen new-venture ideas. A companion tracker (AI_Security_Startup_Tracker.xlsx) contains the structured data and a weekly log that will be appended every Monday.

Three events have defined the 2024-2026 AI-security cycle. First, the unprecedented $32B agreement for Google to acquire Wiz in March 2025 reset the bar for platform consolidation. Second, Palo Alto Networks' acquisition of Protect AI (closed July 2025) and Cato Networks' first-ever acquisition of Aim Security (September 2025) signaled that platform vendors will buy rather than build AI-specific security. Third, Cyera's $540M Series E at a $6B valuation (June 2025) confirmed that data security for AI is the breakout category.

On the offensive side, purpose-built AI red teams and autonomous pen-testers (Haize Labs, Grey Swan, XBOW, SplxAI) are scaling alongside AI-SOC analysts (Dropzone, Prophet, Radiant, Simbian, Crogl). The agentic security layer is emerging but has not yet produced a durable category leader.

2. Methodology

Companies were selected if they (a) apply AI or ML as a first-class capability to a security problem, or (b) provide security for AI systems themselves. Pure-play AI infrastructure vendors without a security angle and pure-play security vendors without an AI angle were excluded.

Funding figures are drawn from company press releases, investor announcements, and tier-one reporting (Reuters, TechCrunch, SC World, CRN). Where the public record is thin, the tracker marks values as "n/d" (not disclosed). Valuations are post-money unless noted. The data is refreshed weekly via a scheduled task.

8. Unique approaches and differentiators

  • Reasoning-first vs. rule-first SOC: Dropzone and Prophet take an agent-reasoning approach with minimal pre-built playbooks, while Simbian and Andesite combine rule graphs with LLM reasoning. Torq and Tines extend legacy SOAR with AI-native actions.

  • Inline vs. air-gapped guardrails: Prompt Security and Lakera emphasize inline guardrails for live traffic. Protect AI (now PANW) and Cranium lean heavier on AI-BOM, scanning, and governance workflows.

  • Data-first vs. identity-first security for AI: Cyera and Sentra argue the control plane is data. Astrix, Oasis, Token, and Clutch argue it is non-human identity. Both will coexist, but consolidation into platform vendors is expected.

  • Autonomous pentest vs. continuous red team: Horizon3 and XBOW aim for full-autonomy adversaries against production targets. Haize Labs, Grey Swan, and SplxAI focus on continuous adversarial evaluation of models.

  • Detection vs. provenance for deepfakes: Reality Defender and Clarity chase better detection. GetReal (Hany Farid) bets on C2PA provenance becoming the standard. Adaptive Security wraps detection into social-engineering training and response.

  • Open-core vs. closed SaaS: Semgrep, Arize (Phoenix), Promptfoo, Socket, Endor, and Chainguard have sizable OSS footprints that drive enterprise deals. Closed SaaS incumbents like Abnormal and Material are the counter-strategy.

  • Platform absorption vs. specialist: Platform vendors (CrowdStrike, Palo Alto, Cisco, Microsoft) are bundling AI-security into existing suites. Specialists must prove that an independent category wedge survives.

9. Ideas to pursue

1. Agentic IAM / delegated authorization for AI agents

Today's IAM (Okta, Entra) was designed for humans and service accounts. AI agents need scoped, delegated, auditable capabilities per tool call, with step-up approvals and consent ceremonies. Wedge is to deeply integrate with agent frameworks (LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen) and offer a policy engine that reasons about capabilities, not just APIs.

2. AI-native insider risk

When employees hand work to AI copilots and agents, the copilot's actions become the attack surface. Classic IRM vendors (DTEX, Forcepoint) don't model agent context or lineage. Build an IRM that unifies user activity, copilot prompts, agent tool calls, and data lineage to score risk in real time.

3. Deepfake defense for call centers and banking ops

Voice vishing of CFOs and wire-approval desks is a weekly headline. KYC and liveness vendors fixed onboarding but not ongoing voice channels. Opportunity: a liveness + behavioral fusion layer that plugs into Genesys, NICE, Five9, and Zoom Contact Center, with an enterprise control plane.

4. Security copilot for SWE agents

Devin-class SWE agents execute shells, open PRs, and touch production systems. Enterprises will not deploy them without a guardrail layer: policy for allowed commands, code classification, PR review, and secret-scoping. Distribute as VS Code extension + GitHub App + CI plugin.

5. AI supply-chain posture (AI-BOM + provenance)

Almost no enterprise can answer 'what model, dataset, and fine-tune is in production, and how was it built.' EU AI Act Article 50 and US sector regs will force this. Build AI-BOM + provenance (C2PA) with a continuous-monitoring layer.

6. AI-native DFIR

IR playbooks remain manual; breaches like MGM, Change Healthcare, and Snowflake revealed how costly this is. Build a DFIR platform that reconstructs attacker TTPs via AI reasoning over telemetry, offers on-call AI responders, and runs as an IR-retainer SaaS with a services overlay.

7. Governed retrieval for enterprise AI

RAG pipelines routinely leak entitlements because document ACLs are stripped during vectorization. Build an ACL-aware retrieval layer and policy engine that plugs into Azure AI Search, OpenSearch, Pinecone, Weaviate, and the major Copilot surfaces.

8. Continuous AI red team as a service

Snapshot assessments are obsolete for systems whose prompts and models change weekly. Deliver continuous red teaming combining a prompt corpus, model of the app, eval harness, and attack agents. Sell into regulated industries with EU AI Act reporting obligations.

9. Small-business AI phishing defense

Abnormal and Material are enterprise-priced. With AI-phish attacking SMBs, MSPs need a defense they can bundle. Build MSP-first phishing defense with per-seat pricing, native Microsoft 365 / Google Workspace coverage, and a QR/SMS/voice layer.

10. Agent-aware secrets vending

Agents read repos and CI. Their blast radius is bigger than a human developer's. Build a secrets vending layer that issues short-lived, scoped credentials per agent invocation, with detection when an agent deviates from its declared scope.

11. EU AI Act compliance automation

GPAI model risk classification, documentation, post-market monitoring, and serious-incident reporting become mandatory in 2026. Build a platform with prebuilt taxonomies, cross-framework mappings (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act), and continuous evidence generation.

12. Browser-based AI employee DLP

Employees paste secrets into ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. Network DLP misses in-browser data, and CASBs trail the long tail of AI apps. Build a managed browser + Chrome/Edge extension + LLM classifier that covers the inference channel.

13. Synthetic-media KYC and liveness

Incode, Jumio, and Onfido were built before voice/video injection attacks matured. Build sensor-fusion liveness (camera, accelerometer, microphone, active challenges) and a provenance backbone for onboarding, SIM swap, and 3DS flows.

14. AI threat-intel fusion

CTI products remain document-centric. Build an AI layer that fuses OSINT, dark-web, vuln data, and industry feeds into per-customer narratives, with agents that refine queries and produce briefs. Partner with IR firms and MSSPs for distribution.

15. Policy-as-code for AI agents

Rego/OPA was not designed for agent decision paths. Create a policy runtime that reasons about agent plans, tool calls, and data lineage, with simulation, a 'deny-explain' UX, and auditing built in.

Exits & M&A

  • 2026-05-04 — Cisco announces intent to acquire Astrix Security for ~$400M (per Calcalist, Globes, Times of Israel). Astrix (founded 2021 by Unit 8200 vets Alon Jackson + Idan Gour) had raised >$85M from Bessemer / CRV / Menlo Ventures and was last marked at ~$200M. The deal will fold Astrix into Cisco Identity Intelligence, Secure Access, and Duo to discover, govern, and secure AI agents and non-human identities. Strategic significance: this is the largest pure-play NHI exit to date, and the first $400M+ outcome explicitly priced on the "secure the agentic workforce" thesis. Implication: platform vendors (Cisco, Palo Alto, CrowdStrike, Microsoft, ServiceNow, Okta) will likely race to lock down their NHI / agent-IAM stack — expect Oasis, Token, Astrix-class peers to see follow-on platform-vendor interest within 1–2 quarters.

  • 2026-05-07 — NextPoint Group (Godspeed Capital portfolio) acquires UScontracting to expand intelligence-focused capabilities (cybersecurity, signals intelligence, data analysis) for U.S. national-security customers. Terms not disclosed. Significance: govcon roll-up rather than a pure AI+security signal, but reinforces the broader IC / defense pull on cyber consolidation.

New entrants and incumbent product launches

  • Microsoft Agent 365 — GA on 2026-05-01. Microsoft's control plane for AI agents went general availability for Commercial customers at $15/user/month (or bundled in the new Microsoft 365 E7 "Frontier Suite"). Agent 365 assigns each AI agent its own Entra identity, applies Purview labels, governs runtime behavior through Defender, extends Intune device management to local agents on Windows, and ships registry sync with AWS Bedrock and Google Cloud Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform (multicloud governance). Implication: every AI-agent-IAM startup now competes directly with a productized Microsoft offering at $15/user; differentiation must come from non-Microsoft estate coverage, deeper runtime, or skill / tool inspection that Microsoft does not yet ship.

  • Netskope AgentSkope — launched 2026-05-06. New AI agent foundation inside Netskope One Platform that automates SOC / NOC alert triage, investigations, policy support, configuration checks, troubleshooting, and risk queries. Significance: another incumbent (Netskope) joins the agentic SOC race; pressure on pure-play AI-SOC startups (Dropzone, Prophet, Radiant, Simbian, 7AI) intensifies — incumbents bundle agents into existing platforms, removing buying friction.

  • ThreatBook Flocks + SafeSkill — launched 2026-05-07. Flocks is an AI-native SecOps platform; SafeSkill inspects and evaluates AI-agent skills (pre-import inspection, marketplace filtering, download scanning, inventory remediation to detect tampering or hidden risks). Significance: SafeSkill is the first material productization of a new category — AI agent skill / tool marketplace security — that we are now adding to our themes list. ThreatBook is a Beijing-based incumbent; the equivalent net-new Western startup play is wide open.

Mid-market and SMB segments will adopt the Microsoft default; the pure-play opportunity narrows to (a) non-Microsoft-estate coverage, (b) advanced runtime / skill inspection, and (c) regulated-industry differentiation.

Weekly Update — Week of 2026-05-04

Coverage window: 2026-04-28 to 2026-05-04 (normal weekly cadence; previous successful run was 2026-04-27). The headline this week is that “AI Gateway” has been formalized as a category by Palo Alto Networks, frontier AI labs are now central to U.S. cyber policy, and defenders shipped multiple agentic-AI countermeasures the same week Anthropic disclosed Mythos’s near-instant exploitation capability. Pure AI+security venture financing was quiet in the U.S. window; the activity moved to platform M&A, partnerships, and product launches.

Headline events

On April 30, Palo Alto Networks announced its intent to acquire Portkey, an AI Gateway provider already processing trillions of agent tokens per month, and used the deal to declare the AI Gateway the “mission-critical control plane” for autonomous agents. Portkey will become the AI Gateway for Prisma AIRS, layering identity, semantic routing, audit, and policy controls over every agent-to-agent and agent-to-tool interaction. Financial terms were not disclosed; the transaction is expected to close in PANW’s FQ4 FY26. The same day, PANW announced a strategic partnership with Armadin, the new offensive-security company founded by ex-Mandiant CEO Kevin Mandia, to embed Armadin’s autonomous AI attack swarm into Unit 42’s Frontier AI Defense service for continuous external attack validation. Combined with the previously closed Koi acquisition (April 14, ∼$400M) and the Chronosphere close (January 29, $3.35B), PANW is now the most aggressive platform consolidator in agentic AI security, spanning gateway, endpoint, observability, and offensive validation.

The other major event from the prior week that needed to be captured for completeness: ServiceNow closed its $7.75B acquisition of Armis on April 20, the largest pure cybersecurity exit since the Google–Wiz close, and the largest AI-cyber deal centered on OT/IoT exposure management. ServiceNow leadership publicly framed the combination as a path to “the largest cyber business in the world.”

On the policy and frontier-model front: Axios reported that OpenAI and Anthropic each held closed-door classified briefings with the House Homeland Security Committee on the cyber-offensive capabilities of their newest models. Anthropic delayed Mythos Preview because the model can rapidly identify and exploit vulnerabilities; OpenAI moved to a tiered release for GPT-5.4-Cyber. On May 1 the Pentagon announced classified-network deployment agreements (IL6/IL7) with eight firms — AWS, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, SpaceX, NVIDIA, Reflection, and Oracle — with Anthropic notably excluded over a usage-policy dispute around domestic surveillance and autonomous weapons. The list confirms a clear “sovereign AI” market that frontier-model and platform vendors are racing to occupy, and creates a meaningful commercial penalty for safety-policy stringency.

New entrants and notable launches

Sevii launched its Cyber Swarm Defense (CSD) mode on April 28 inside its autonomous Defense and Remediation (ADR) platform. The notable design choice is commercial, not technical: CSD charges by asset protected, not by AI-token consumption, and uses Sevii’s “Myrmidon Defense Technology” to dynamically scale agent fleets while guaranteeing sub-15-minute remediation SLOs. This addresses the single biggest objection enterprise buyers have to agentic AI security — unbounded token cost — and we expect copy-cat pricing models from Dropzone, Prophet, and Simbian over the next quarter.

On April 30, Anthropic launched Claude Security in public beta, accessible from claude.ai/security. The product is a vulnerability scanner built on Opus 4.7 that reasons over entire codebases, traces data flow, attaches confidence ratings, and ships with a multi-stage validation pipeline to suppress false positives. CrowdStrike, Palo Alto Networks, SentinelOne, Trend Micro (TrendAI), and Wiz are integrating Opus 4.7 into their platforms in parallel. This makes Anthropic both a foundation-model supplier and a direct cyber product vendor, and accelerates the “every model lab is also a security vendor” thesis we have been tracking since late 2025.

On May 1, Cisco open-sourced its Model Provenance Kit (MPK) on GitHub, with an initial fingerprint database of approximately 150 base models from 45 families and 20 publishers, accessible on Hugging Face. MPK uses architecture metadata, tokenizer structure, and weight-level signals to determine if two transformer models share a common origin. The release validates AI supply-chain integrity as a real customer problem and creates the de facto schema future commercial products will need to interoperate with.

Themes — what shifted this week

AI Gateway is now a named category. PANW’s Portkey acquisition follows the same pattern as “Agentic Endpoint Security” with Koi: name a category, integrate the asset into Prisma AIRS, and force every other platform vendor to respond. Watch for Microsoft (Azure AI Foundry / Copilot), Google (Vertex), CrowdStrike (Charlotte), and Cisco to ship or buy a comparable gateway in the next two quarters.

Frontier AI labs are now cyber product vendors. Anthropic Claude Security (April 30) is a direct, branded vulnerability-scanning product, not just a model API. OpenAI’s tiered GPT-5.4-Cyber release follows the same direction. The implication for security startups: any product whose moat is “we wrote good prompts on top of a frontier model” is now competing with the frontier lab itself. Defensible wedges shift to proprietary telemetry, vertical integration, customer-tenant data residency, and policy/governance.

Sovereign AI is bifurcating along policy lines. Pentagon’s IL6/IL7 list (AWS, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, SpaceX, NVIDIA, Reflection, Oracle) confirms a 7–8-vendor public-sector AI oligopoly. Anthropic’s exclusion creates a clear two-track market: vendors willing to ship with broad-use clauses for defense customers, and vendors with stricter usage policies that will be limited to commercial. This reinforces our Idea #17 thesis (sovereign agentic SOC) and increases the value of model-agnostic agent runtimes.

AI supply-chain integrity moves from theoretical to operational. Cisco’s open-sourced Model Provenance Kit gives defenders a usable primitive for model lineage. EU AI Act enforcement and SBOM-for-AI mandates are now arriving with practical tooling. We are adding this as a new fundable wedge (Idea #20).

Time-to-exploit collapses to hours. Anthropic’s Mythos disclosure, the GitHub CVE-2026-3854 RCE, and SecurityWeek’s “industrial cybercrime” analysis (April 30) all point to the same operational reality. BCG’s April 29 piece argues the gap is no longer tooling but synchronization: business, IT, and security must coordinate at AI speed. This favors platform vendors with end-to-end visibility (PANW, ServiceNow, Microsoft) and squeezes specialist point products that cannot integrate across the operating model.
